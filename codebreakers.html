Colossus

Colossus was the name of a series of computers invented by British codebreakers between 1943-1945. These computers helped to win World War II by translating ("cracking") secret coded messages sent between German military leaders and their troops.

Colossus computers were the first electronic programmable computers. "Electronic" means all their parts involved electrical circuits than mechanical gears and levers. "Programmable" meant that the computer could behave in different ways based on the program they loaded. (Earlier computers could only behave in one way!)

Colossus computers were different from our modern computers, though. Although they could behave in different ways, they could only do one kind of task — codebreaking. Also, they had no screens and no keyboards where you could type or create a program for them to run. Instead, people programmed them by moving wires among different switches on the outside of the computer. (The term "hardwired" is related to this idea.) Colossus computers printed their results on an electric typewriter.
